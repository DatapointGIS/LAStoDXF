Export
DXF is written in the cloud's native coordinates as closed polylines, one CAD layer per extraction. Open directly in Civil 3D, then SAVEAS → .dwg. The WKID is recorded as a DXF comment; keep the .prj next to the DXF and ArcGIS will read the coordinate system automatically. True .dwg is a closed binary format and can't be written in a browser.
Method & limits

What this does: bins the cloud to an elevation grid, masks cells by your rule (height-above-ground, elevation band, LAS class, or color match), optionally re-fits the mask boundary to the ortho, cleans the mask, traces region boundaries, and simplifies them into closed polylines.

What it is not: a replacement for TopoDOT / TerraScan-style feature extraction. It produces draft outlines (building footprints, canopy edges, pads, ponds) that a tech should review and square up in CAD.

Imagery in processing: a georeferenced ortho (JPG/PNG + ESRI world file, or GeoTIFF) loaded in panel 2 is resampled onto the processing grid and used two ways: Snap edges to imagery migrates the lidar mask boundary to color edges in the photo within your search distance (cells deeper inside or outside are frozen, so the photo refines edges but cannot invent features), and Color match can run entirely off ortho colors when the cloud carries no RGB. The ortho must share the cloud’s CRS and units; the E/N registration shift is honored during processing, and rotated world files are placed without the rotation. The photo also draws under the linework for QC.

Editing: turn on editing in the right panel, then drag square handles to move vertices, drag hollow midpoints to add them, click a line to select it, and use the Delete buttons (or Del) to remove vertices, lines, or whole paths. Removing a line opens a closed outline into a polyline; removing one from an open path splits it — open paths export as open polylines in the DXF. Ctrl+Z undoes the last 60 edits.

LAZ files: decompress first — ArcGIS Pro (Convert LAS), las2las, or PDAL — then load the .las here. Everything runs locally in the browser; the only exception is the GeoTIFF reader library, fetched from a CDN the first time a .tif is dropped. Imagery itself never leaves the machine.
